## 1. Leadlines

Every content slide should have a **leadline** (also called a "ledeline") — a sentence at the top of the slide that states the main takeaway.

**Rules:**
- The leadline should be a complete sentence that communicates the key insight
- Leadlines should connect horizontally across the deck to tell a coherent story
- Reading just the leadlines should convey the full argument

**Good leadlines** (from real consulting slides):
- "As we zero in on total customer employees, current ranges imply both an ELA TAM and break-even prices"
- "TAM and break-even lines suggest both opportunity and price level requirements"
- "A clear solution pathway will be needed to entice upgrade for customers consuming beneath ELA level"
- "At the optimal price, the net difference between 'Pick Two' and 'Unified IT' is marginal"
- "Price response strategies imply the company should price below the Microsoft 3-year TCO in most pricing ranges"

**Bad leadlines:** Titles that merely label the content (e.g., "Revenue Chart" or "Customer Analysis")

### Leadlines vs. Graph Titles
Two different text elements that serve different purposes:
- **Leadline** (top of slide): An analytical statement — the key insight or takeaway. Example: "Adoption rate assumptions have large impact on uplift"
- **Graph title** (on the chart itself): A descriptive label — what the chart shows. Example: "Expected Revenue by Adoption Scenario"

Leadlines tell you WHAT TO THINK. Graph titles tell you WHAT YOU'RE LOOKING AT. Never confuse the two.

**Leadline rules (enforced in SKILL.md Phase 3 Step 5 and Phase 4 Step 2):**
- Leadlines must be positive analytical claims, not testable hypotheses
- No hedge language ("sufficient," "adequate," "may," "could")
- No embedded statistics — stats belong in the slide body, not the leadline
- Test: reading only the leadlines should tell the full recommendation story

## 2. Graph and Chart Rules

### Axis Labels
- Every axis must be clearly labeled with the variable name and units
- Include currency symbols, percentage signs, or units as appropriate

### Data Labels
- Include data labels on key data points
- For stacked charts, label both individual segments and totals
- Use callout boxes for important values

### Chart Types by Purpose

| Purpose | Recommended Chart |
|---|---|
| Compare magnitudes | Bar/column chart |
| Compare proportions | Stacked 100% bar |
| Show trends | Line chart |
| Show correlation | Scatter plot |
| Show composition breakdown | Waterfall chart |
| Show cumulative distribution | Pareto (line) |
| Compare few categories with totals | Stacked column (absolute) |
| Qualitative scoring | Harvey Balls matrix |

### Source Lines
- Every data-driven slide should have a source line at the bottom
- Format: "Source: [specific source], [year]"
- Include methodology notes where relevant

### Direct Labeling
Prefer labeling parts of the graph directly over using a legend. Direct labels reduce eye movement and make the chart immediately readable. Use a legend only when direct labeling would create clutter (e.g., 6+ data series).

## 3. Footnotes and Sources

- Place footnotes at the bottom of the slide in smaller font
- Number footnotes with superscript numbers (1, 2, 3...)
- Include data caveats, methodology notes, and definitions
- Mark illustrative or estimated data explicitly: "I L L U S T R A T I V E" or "Estimated"

## 4. Slide Layout Principles

### Structure
- One key message per slide (expressed in the leadline)
- Supporting evidence below (chart, table, or bullets)
- Source and footnotes at bottom
- Considerations / implications in a sidebar or callout box when needed

### Callout Section Naming
The right-side callout section should be labeled with action-oriented titles:
- "Key Implications" — for strategic conclusions drawn from the data
- "Key Takeaways" — for summary points
- "Considerations" — for risks, caveats, or decision factors
Provide enough text to explain but not so much that the slide becomes cluttered.

### Visual Hierarchy
- Leadline: largest/boldest text after title
- Chart/visual: dominant center area
- Annotations: smaller, supporting text
- Footnotes/sources: smallest

### Professional Formatting
- Use consistent color scheme across the deck
- Align elements to a grid
- Keep font sizes consistent by element type
- Use white space effectively — don't overcrowd

## 5. Professional Slide Examples

The following types of slides appear frequently in consulting-style case presentations:

### Pricing Analysis Slide
- Leadline about pricing implication
- Chart showing price vs. customer segments
- TAM line and breakeven line overlaid
- Footnotes with assumptions
- Source line

### Customer Segmentation Slide
- Histogram of customers by price tier
- Callout boxes showing % that would pay more/less
- "Considerations" sidebar listing strategic implications
- Candidate solutions listed

### Scenario Comparison Slide
- Side-by-side or overlaid charts showing multiple scenarios
- Clear labeling of "High adoption" vs "Low adoption" etc.
- Implications box
- Detailed footnotes on model assumptions

### Impact Waterfall Slide
- Shows base → adjustments → total in waterfall format
- Dollar amounts labeled on each bar
- Assumptions listed below
- Clear story: "This strategy produces $X uplift"

## 6. Implication Writing Standards

Every implication on a slide must be a **strategic conclusion**, not a data restatement.

**Bad:** "Revenue increased 15% year over year" (this is data — it belongs in the chart)
**Good:** "Pick Two pricing could lead to a larger uplift if significant step-ups occur" (this is a strategic conclusion)
**Good:** "Higher adoption rates call for higher relative prices at the optimum since customers are more willing to switch" (this connects data to a decision)

Each implication should answer: "So what? What should the decision-maker DO with this information?"

## 7. Sensitivity Analysis Presentation

When presenting model output, identify and highlight the most sensitive assumption:

1. **Identify** the assumption that most changes the outcome when varied
2. **Show the range** — present multiple scenarios side by side (e.g., high/low adoption)
3. **State the strategic implication** — what does the sensitivity tell the decision-maker?

Example pattern: Three charts side by side showing the same output metric under different assumption values, with a leadline like "Adoption rate assumptions have large impact on uplift but less significant impact on penetration."

## 8. Recommendation Slide

Every case deck must end with a recommendation slide. The format is principle-based:

### Principles
- State a clear recommendation with supporting rationale
- Include decision criteria so the audience understands WHY this option wins
- Address key risks or contingencies

### When Multiple Viable Options Exist
Present numbered options with:
- Key metrics for each (pricing, expected impact, etc.)
- "Choose this if..." decision criteria per option
- A framing statement if differences are marginal

### When One Clear Winner Exists
- Direct recommendation statement
- Top 2-3 supporting reasons
- Key risks and how they're mitigated
- Next steps for implementation
