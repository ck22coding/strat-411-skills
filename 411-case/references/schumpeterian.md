## Why Standard Economics Falls Short

Standard equilibrium economics cannot explain the dynamics of modern competitive markets. Two worldviews compared:

| Dimension | Neo-Classical Assumptions | Schumpeterian Reality |
|-----------|--------------------------|----------------------|
| **Competition** | Price competition generates the greatest value for society | New products provide greater social value than price competition alone; pure price competition often does not prevail in practice |
| **Equilibrium** | Economic forces move instantly to equilibrium | Economic forces never fully reach equilibrium — they evolve |
| **Decision-making** | Firms maximize profits; consumers maximize utility | Firms and consumers "satisfice" rather than optimize |
| **Knowledge** | Decisions use complete knowledge of options and outcomes | Firms that earn rents lose them over time to innovators |

Standard equilibrium theory handles the question of economic order (how activity is coordinated without central authority) but cannot deal with change — especially the disequilibrium dynamics of industries like computers, pharmaceuticals, or media.

## The Creative Destruction Cycle

> "The fundamental impulse that sets and keeps the capitalist engine in motion comes from the new consumers' goods, the new methods of production or transportation, the new markets, the new forms of industrial organization that capitalist enterprise creates... This process of Creative Destruction is the essential fact about capitalism. It is what capitalism consists in and what every capitalist concern has got to live in."
> — Joseph Schumpeter, *Capitalism, Socialism and Democracy*, p. 83

> "The essential point to grasp is that in dealing with capitalism we are dealing with an evolutionary process. It may seem strange that anyone can fail to see so obvious a fact... yet that fragmentary analysis which yields the bulk of our propositions about the functioning of modern capitalism persistently neglects it."
> — Joseph Schumpeter, *Capitalism, Socialism and Democracy*, p. 82

### Three Phases

1. **Periods of Quiet** — Leading firms earn economic rent. Stable market structures persist. Incumbents defend their position.
2. **Periods of Shock** — New sources of advantage emerge. Old advantages break down. Disruption reshapes the landscape.
3. **Entrepreneurial Exploitation** — Entrepreneurs seize opportunities. New rent-earning firms emerge. The cycle begins again.

### Self-Destruction Imperative

Firms must creatively destroy their own sources of advantage — or someone else will.

- Waves of Schumpeterian competition arise from innovation from all sources
- Innovation benefits consumers more than price competition
- Rent-earning assets do not hold their value as firms innovate and consumer tastes change

Each successive advantage has a finite lifespan. The firm's economic profits and market share follow a wave pattern: Advantage #1 peaks and declines as Advantage #2 rises, which in turn declines as Advantage #3 emerges.

## Market Regimes

Schumpeter observed two types of competitive regimes that produce fundamentally different market structures.

### Entrepreneurial Regime
- Creative destruction in full operation
- Firms struggle to maintain leadership for more than a few years
- Leadership changes with each new technology wave
- Examples: disk drives, MP3 players, mobile phones (1990s)

### Routinized Regime
- Large firms "automatize" progress
- Cumulative technological effects favor large industrial R&D labs
- Market structures tend to be stable with durable leadership
- Examples: cola, automobiles, telecom

**Key finding:** Small changes in underlying parameters produce dramatically different market structures — from concentrated to fragmented. The same simulation, varying only opportunity, appropriability, cumulativeness, and knowledge-type parameters, produces starkly different industry structures (Source: Winter 1982 simulation model).

### Five Regime Determinants

| Factor | Definition | Routinized | Entrepreneurial |
|--------|-----------|:----------:|:---------------:|
| **Opportunities** | Probability of innovating for a given R&D investment | Low (−) | High (+) |
| **Appropriability** | Strength of IP protection for innovation | Strong + (favors incumbents) | Weak − (favors entrants) |
| **Cumulativeness** | Whether today's innovations build on yesterday's knowledge | High + (incumbents compound advantage) | Low − (fresh starts possible) |
| **Generic Knowledge** | Extent innovations benefit from basic scientific research | High + (favors large R&D labs) | Low (−) |
| **Specific Knowledge** | Extent innovations benefit from applied/experiential research | Low (−) | High + (favors practitioners/startups) |

Source: Breschi et al. (2000)

### Quick Test

High appropriability + high cumulativeness + basic science drives advances → routinized regime.

### Auto Industry: Classic Regime Transition

- **Entrepreneurial Phase (1894-1912):** Rapid entry, 275+ firms competing by 1909, frequent leadership changes, high entry/exit rates.
- **Routinized Phase (post-1930):** Shakeout complete, stable oligopoly of a few dominant firms (GM, Ford, Chrysler).

This is the textbook example of a regime transition — from entrepreneurial chaos to stable oligopoly.

## Theoretical Models

Four complementary theoretical models explain different dimensions of how Schumpeterian competition operates:

### 1. Winter's Technological Regimes (1984)
Simulation model generating patterns of Schumpeterian competition from a small set of underlying parameters. Demonstrates that varying opportunity, appropriability, cumulativeness, and knowledge-type parameters produces starkly different industry structures — from entrepreneurial fragmentation to routinized concentration.

### 2. Nelson & Winter's Evolutionary Theory (1982)
Firms as bundles of routines; selection and variation drive market evolution. Organizational memory is encoded in behavior patterns. Routines are the analogue of individual skill — firms behave according to routines employed in the past. Competence is confined to quasi-repetitive situations.

### 3. Christensen's Disruption Model (1997)
Low-end and new-market disruption. Incumbents systematically overshoot customer needs by pursuing sustaining innovations, leaving them vulnerable to entrants who serve overlooked segments with simpler, cheaper offerings that improve over time. Disruption originates from below or from new markets where incumbents are absent.

### 4. Christensen, Bryce & Dyer: High-End Disruption (2006)
High-end disruptive innovations introduce a **discontinuity in performance at mass-market introduction**. Unlike low-end disruption (which starts inferior and improves), high-end disruption enters above the range of performance that customers demand, creating a step-change that incumbents cannot match. This extends Christensen's original model by showing disruption attacks from above as well as below.

## Disruption Taxonomy

Two axes define four quadrants:
- **X-axis:** Established Consumption ←→ New Consumption
- **Y-axis:** Relative Performance at Introduction (Low ←→ High)

(Performance = performance of new offering at introduction relative to average performance demanded by consumers in established technology.)

| Quadrant | Performance | Market | Why Incumbents Miss It | Examples |
|----------|:----------:|--------|----------------------|----------|
| **Sustaining Innovations** | High | Established | They don't — incumbents usually win here | Incremental improvements to existing products |
| **Low-End Disruptive** | Low | Established | Looks **unattractive** — "why serve the worst customers?" | Nucor, Southwest, Charles Schwab, Fuji single-use camera |
| **New-Market Disruptive** | Low | New | Looks **unattainable/inaccessible** — "that's not our market" | PC, Palm Pilot, eBay, digital camera |
| **High-End Disruptive** | High | New | Discontinuity in performance at mass-market introduction | Starbucks, Apple iPod, FedEx, JetBlue, fuel injection systems |

### High-End Disruption Detail

Christensen's original 1997 model shows sustaining innovations overshooting customer needs while low-end disruptive innovations creep upward from below. The 2006 extension (Christensen, Bryce & Dyer) adds high-end disruptive innovations that enter **above** the range of performance customers demand, creating a discontinuity — a step-change in performance — at the point of mass-market introduction. This means disruption attacks incumbents from **both ends** simultaneously: low-end erosion from below and high-end leapfrogging from above.

"What will AI disrupt — and high end or low end?" This is the open strategic question facing every industry. Apply the taxonomy: Is AI entering as a low-end disruption (cheaper/simpler substitute for existing consumption) or a high-end disruption (step-change in performance creating new consumption)?

## Strategic Imperatives

1. **Competition is rarely stable** — Threats, perceived or real, constantly condition the moves of strategic actors. Stability is temporary.
2. **Some industries achieve prolonged stability** — Industries may reach stability for long periods, thwarting appropriation of returns — at least until innovations are truly radical enough.
3. **Effective actors must choose their strategy** — Be a good innovator, or a masterful obstructionist by creating barriers to your own demise. Passive actors lose.

**The only strategic choice is whether to be the disruptor or the disrupted.**

## Key Examples

### Typesetter Industry
Four successive technology waves completely replaced each other over 40 years — a textbook example of creative destruction:
1. Hot Metal
2. Analog Photosetter
3. Digital CRT
4. Laser Imagesetter

Each new technology completely displaced the last — **no incumbents survived all four transitions.**

Source: Tripsas (1997), "Unraveling the Process of Creative Destruction," *Strategic Management Journal* 18: 119-142.

### Gaming Consoles
The gaming console market exemplifies entrepreneurial-regime competition — each generation's leader is displaced by the next wave of innovation. Chart data (1989-2019) shows successive waves:
- Sega Genesis / Super NES / NEC TurboGrafx-16
- Nintendo 64 / Sega Saturn / Atari Jaguar / Sony PlayStation
- PlayStation 2 / Sega Dreamcast / Xbox / Nintendo GameCube
- Nintendo Wii / Xbox 360 / PlayStation 3
- PlayStation 4 / Nintendo Switch / Xbox One / Nintendo Wii U

No single firm maintains leadership across generations. Each wave's winner is typically displaced by the next.

Source: Statista (1989-2019)

### Netflix vs. Video Rental
Netflix streaming destroyed the 28,000-store US video rental industry in under 15 years — a textbook case of Schumpeterian creative destruction:
- **27,882** — Peak rental stores (2000)
- **409** — Stores remaining (2025)
- **98.5%** — Stores lost since 2000
- **260M+** — Netflix subscribers (2023)

Netflix pre-2007 = DVD-by-mail subscribers; post-2007 = streaming.

Sources: Wikipedia, IBISWorld, Netflix Investor Relations, Statista

### Auto Industry
- **Entrepreneurial Phase (1894-1912):** 275+ firms competing by 1909. Rapid entry, frequent leadership changes, high entry/exit rates.
- **Routinized Phase (post-1930):** Shakeout complete, stable oligopoly (GM, Ford, Chrysler).

Classic regime transition driven by increasing cumulativeness (manufacturing process knowledge), rising appropriability (scale-based cost advantages), and growing dependence on generic/scientific knowledge (materials science, engineering).

## Internal Questions Claude Should Ask

### Regime Classification
- Which regime is this industry in — entrepreneurial or routinized? Use the five factors to classify.
- Is the industry transitioning from one regime to another (like auto in 1910-1930)?

### Appropriability & Cumulativeness
- How easy is it for competitors to copy innovations (appropriability)?
- Does success build on itself, or can newcomers start fresh (cumulativeness)?
- Is the knowledge base scientific/generic or applied/experiential?

### Disruption Direction
- Is disruption coming from the low end, high end, or new market?
- Is there a discontinuity in performance at mass-market introduction (high-end disruption signal)?
- What will AI disrupt in this industry — and from which direction?

### Strategic Posture
- Is the firm being a good innovator or a good obstructionist — or neither (passive)?
- Is the firm creatively destroying its own advantages before someone else does?
- What rent-earning assets are at risk of losing value?

### Landscape Connection
- Is the landscape shifting? Which firms face the greatest fall?
- Are the firm's routines (Layer 2A) preventing it from responding to creative destruction?
- Is this a case where the very competencies that made the firm successful are now blocking adaptation?
