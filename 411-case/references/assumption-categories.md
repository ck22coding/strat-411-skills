# Assumption Categories for the Quick Dirty Test

When identifying assumptions for a candidate solution, work through each category below and generate 1-2 assumptions per relevant category, aiming for 8-10 total. Not every category applies to every solution — skip categories that clearly don't apply, and let the user add categories or assumptions that are missing.

---

## 1. Market Assumptions
Questions to ask: Does the target market exist? Is there demand? Is the market growing or shrinking? How big is the addressable market?

**Example assumptions:**
- "There is sufficient demand for [product/service] in [target market]"
- "The total addressable market is at least $X"
- "The market is growing at X% annually"
- "Market conditions will remain stable enough for this strategy to work"

---

## 2. Financial Assumptions
Questions to ask: Are the economics viable? Can we achieve target margins? Is the required investment reasonable? What's the expected ROI and payback period?

**Example assumptions:**
- "The company can achieve a gross margin of at least X%"
- "Revenue will cover the required upfront investment within X years"
- "The cost structure allows profitable operation at expected volume"
- "Financing for the required investment is available at reasonable rates"

---

## 3. Operational Assumptions
Questions to ask: Can the company actually execute this? Is the timeline realistic? Are supply chains adequate? Can processes be built or adapted? Has the firm tried similar changes before? What happened? How deeply embedded are the processes that would need to change?

**Example assumptions:**
- "The company can operationally execute this within X months/years"
- "Supply chain capacity exists to support the required volume"
- "Existing processes can be adapted without major disruption"
- "Implementation can happen without degrading current business performance"

---

## 4. Resource & Capability Assumptions
Questions to ask: Does the company have the people, technology, and skills needed? Can missing capabilities be acquired or built in time? Could the firm's existing competencies actually block this change? Are the routines that make the firm good at X the same ones that would prevent Y?

**Example assumptions:**
- "The company has (or can hire) the talent required"
- "The necessary technology exists and is accessible"
- "Required capabilities can be built or acquired within the timeline"
- "The organization can absorb the change without losing key personnel"
- "The capabilities that made the company successful in its current market won't hinder adaptation to the new approach"
- "The organization can unlearn existing processes without losing the competence those processes provide"

---

## 5. Competitive Assumptions
Questions to ask: How will competitors respond? Is the advantage sustainable? Can competitors replicate this easily?

**Example assumptions:**
- "Competitors will not effectively replicate this strategy within X years"
- "The company's competitive advantage in [area] is durable"
- "Competitor response will not erode expected market share gains"
- "First-mover advantage exists and is meaningful in this market"

---

## 6. Customer Assumptions
Questions to ask: Will customers adopt? Are they willing to pay the target price? What are switching costs? Is the value proposition compelling?

**Example assumptions:**
- "Customers are willing to pay $X for [product/service]"
- "Customer acquisition cost is below $X"
- "Customer switching costs from current alternatives are low enough to enable adoption"
- "Customer retention rate will be at least X% after initial adoption"

---

## 7. Brand & Positioning Assumptions
Questions to ask: Can the brand credibly extend into this space? Will the move affect perception of the core brand? Is the positioning differentiated?

**Example assumptions:**
- "The company's brand can credibly extend to [new category/market]"
- "This move will not dilute the core brand's value proposition"
- "The positioning is sufficiently differentiated from competitors"
- "Target customers will perceive the brand favorably in this new context"

---

## 8. Regulatory & Legal Assumptions
Questions to ask: Are there regulatory barriers? Can compliance be achieved? Are there intellectual property concerns? What's the legal risk?

**Example assumptions:**
- "No regulatory barriers prevent entry into [market/geography]"
- "Compliance with [specific regulations] is achievable and affordable"
- "Intellectual property risks are manageable"
- "The regulatory environment will remain stable over the planning horizon"

---

## How to Use These Categories

1. **Generate**: For each selected candidate solution, work through the categories and generate 1-2 assumptions per relevant category
2. **Present**: Organize assumptions by category when showing them to the user
3. **Customize**: Ask the user if there are additional categories specific to their case (e.g., "Technology risk," "Partnership assumptions," "Cultural/organizational assumptions")
4. **Prioritize**: Some assumptions are more critical than others — flag the ones where failure would completely invalidate the solution
5. **Test**: Apply the QDT logic — if any critical assumption is likely false, the solution should be reconsidered
