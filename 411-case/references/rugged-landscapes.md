## Part 1: Fitness Landscapes

The spatial metaphor underlying all strategic reasoning in this course. The "landscape metaphor" visualizes strategy as a topographic surface where elevation represents performance (fitness). Firms occupy positions on this landscape defined by their configuration of strategic choices. The goal is to reach the highest peak -- but the terrain's shape determines how hard that is.

### NK Model
- **N** = number of distinct attributes/decisions defining the organization (pricing, cost structure, HR, culture, tech platform, distribution, etc.)
- **K** = interdependence level -- how many other elements influence the fitness contribution of any single element
  - K=0: fully independent elements, smooth landscape, single global peak -- optimize each element independently
  - K increases: rugged landscape, many peaks -- changing one element affects K others
  - K=N-1: maximally interdependent, highly rugged, many local peaks -- global optimum nearly impossible to find

### Smooth vs. Rugged: The Role of Interdependence
- **Smooth landscape (K=0):** each element contributes independently to fitness. One clear global peak. Hill-climbing reliably finds the optimum.
- **Rugged landscape (high K):** elements are tightly coupled. Changing one choice ripples through others. The landscape fractures into many peaks separated by valleys of lower performance.
- Interdependence (K) is the single parameter that transforms a simple optimization problem into a strategic dilemma.

### Visualizing Ruggedness: How K Shapes the Landscape
As K rises:
1. **More local peaks** -- more places to get stuck
2. **Lower average peak heights** -- getting trapped is costlier relative to the global optimum
3. **Harder to navigate** -- local search becomes less effective; the distance between current position and the global peak grows

### Five Core Concepts
1. **Performance Peaks** -- specific attribute configurations yielding high fitness; goal is finding the highest
2. **Local Search** -- exploring nearby alternatives via small adjustments; low risk, limited exploration
3. **Hill-Climbing** -- moving incrementally, accepting only fitness-improving changes; simple but can trap
4. **Path Dependence** -- where you end up depends heavily on where you started; history constrains future options
5. **Long Jumps** -- radical, discontinuous reconfigurations abandoning current position; risky but necessary to escape local peaks

### The Local Peak Trap
1. Firm reaches a local peak through incremental improvement
2. Any small change now reduces performance
3. Firm becomes locked into suboptimal strategy
4. Cannot reach higher peak without crossing a valley of lower performance
- "Local peaks feel like success -- but the firm may be dramatically underperforming its potential"

### Escaping the Trap: Long Jumps
Long jumps are radical reconfigurations that abandon the current position entirely. They are costly, uncertain, and often resisted -- but necessary when a firm is trapped on a local peak far below the global optimum.

Examples of long jumps:
- **Complete business model pivots** -- abandoning the current revenue/cost architecture for an entirely new one
- **Wholesale digital transformation** -- replacing legacy systems, processes, and capabilities simultaneously rather than incrementally
- **Market/segment redefinition** -- exiting current customer segments and entering fundamentally different ones

Long jumps require changing multiple interdependent elements simultaneously. The higher K is, the more elements must change in concert, and the riskier the jump becomes.

### Exploration-Exploitation Balance
- Smooth landscape (low K) -> exploit (hill-climb)
- Rugged landscape (high K) -> explore more (long jumps needed because traps are plentiful)

### Seven Implications for Competitive Strategy
1. **Optimize the configuration, not the components** -- fitness comes from how choices interact, not from perfecting any single decision in isolation
2. **Design your search process, not just your strategy** -- you cannot predict the global peak, so how you explore matters more than any one plan
3. **Recognize that incrementalism may converge on mediocrity** -- hill-climbing on a rugged landscape reliably traps you on a local peak where every nearby move looks worse
4. **Treat reconfiguration as a deliberate tool, not a last resort** -- long jumps are costly and uncertain, but the cost of staying trapped compounds over time
5. **Respect that your starting point constrains your destination** -- path dependence channels you toward specific peaks and places strong limits on what is possible without radical attribute changes
6. **Match your degree of coupling to your competitive context** -- tight coupling creates coherence but fragility; loose coupling enables adaptation but breeds resistance to transformation
7. **Prepare for landscape shifts as survival threats, not incremental challenges** -- when the environment redefines the fitness function, firms on the highest peaks face the greatest fall

### Three Strategic Scenarios Where This Applies

| Scenario | Insight |
|----------|---------|
| Industry Disruption | Technology or regulation reshapes the entire landscape (Kodak, Blockbuster) |
| Merger Integration | Path dependence means each firm developed unique solutions; combining does not equal better performance |
| Organizational Redesign | Changing incentives without changing structure/culture fails due to K; must change multiple elements simultaneously |

- Levinthal, D. (1997). "Adaptation on Rugged Landscapes." *Management Science* 43(7): 934-950.

## Part 2: Why Firms Get Stuck (Organizational Rigidity)

**Core insight: Firms are bundles of routines. These routines are both the source of competence and a potential trap.** This section explains the micro-level reason firms become trapped on local peaks: the very routines that got them there resist the changes needed to leave.

### Routines as Organizational DNA
Routines are the analogue of individual skill. Firms behave according to routines employed in the past. Routines are semi-automatic, largely tacit, and change slowly. The terms "core competence," "capabilities," "best practices," and "continuous improvement" all reflect the routines view of the firm. Four properties:

1. **Routines as Genes** -- firms behave according to past routines; organizational memory is encoded in behavior patterns
2. **Knowledge Storage** -- routines store organizational knowledge; relearning has costs that rise as behavior leaves the domain of recent practice. The further a firm departs from what it has recently practiced, the more expensive and uncertain relearning becomes.
3. **Conflict Avoidance** -- routines include ways of coping with organizational conflict; departures provoke heightened anxiety and higher stakes
4. **Cognitive Frameworks** -- inherited strategies and mental models channel choices (e.g., Polaroid's razor/blade model delayed digital camera adoption)

### The Competence Puzzle
"How can organizations be so impressively competent from one perspective, yet so strikingly bounded in their rationality from another?"

Resolution: High competence is achievable where skills and routines can be learned and perfected through practice. But practice-based learning does little to enable sophisticated foresight, logically structured deliberation, or the improvisation of novel action patterns.

Key implication: High competence through learning is confined to quasi-repetitive situations. Firms cannot practice nor invest in everything; being good at some things means necessarily not being good at others.

### Bounded Rationality
- Humans are largely incapable of real-time optimization against extensive variable sets
- Instead they reach forward with intent to improve; efforts are highly path-dependent
- Decision making is deeply embedded in temporal contexts shaped by prior decisions -- the "clean slate" situation rarely happens
- Feasible choice sets are typically tightly bounded -- much more tightly than outsiders suppose

### Three Fundamental Economic Questions
1. **Order** -- How is economic activity coordinated without central authority?
2. **Change** -- How does the economy transform (growing productivity, inventiveness)?
3. **Competence** -- How do organizations know what to do?

Standard equilibrium theory handles Order well but cannot deal with Change -- especially in disequilibrium dynamics of industries like computers or pharmaceuticals.

### Strategy Definition (Evolutionary Frame)
Strategy is concerned with effective management of key firm attributes that cannot be altered quickly, costlessly, or reversibly: capabilities, reputations, scale, key personnel. Strategizing is the opposite of drifting, firefighting, and myopic approaches.

### Key Example: JetBlue Valentine's Day 2007
During an ice storm at JFK, staff continued loading flights per routine. Planes sat on tarmac for hours. System collapsed: hundreds of cancelled flights, lawsuits, CEO departure. The very same routines that shaped JetBlue's strategic success caused the disaster. Routines were optimized for normal operations, not novel situations. When the situation left the domain of recent practice, relearning costs spiked and the organization could not improvise effectively.

## Internal Questions Claude Should Ask

These questions are used internally during case analysis. Never present them as a checklist to students -- use them to shape Socratic questions and guide reasoning.

**Landscape questions:**
- Is the firm on a local peak? How do we know?
- How interdependent are the strategic choices (estimate K)?
- Is incremental improvement sufficient, or is radical reconfiguration needed?
- What is the cost of staying trapped vs. the risk of a long jump?
- If the landscape shifts, which firms face the greatest fall?

**Rigidity questions:**
- What routines does this firm have? What routines would it need? The gap explains why the problem exists.
- Could existing competencies block this change?
- Are the routines that make the firm good at X the same ones preventing Y?
- Can the firm realistically build the needed routines? What is the practice-based learning timeline?
- Does this recommendation require capabilities outside the firm's routine set?
- How far is the proposed change from the firm's domain of recent practice? How steep are relearning costs?
- Is decision making here constrained by temporal context -- what prior decisions box the firm in?
