## The Escape Problem

Routines trap firms on local peaks (see rugged-landscapes.md). Every small change reduces performance, so the firm stays put -- even when dramatically underperforming its potential. This file covers HOW firms escape.

Two escape mechanisms exist: building meta-level capabilities (dynamic capabilities) and making radical jumps to entirely new configurations (long jumps). They are complementary -- dynamic capabilities determine whether a firm CAN execute a long jump.

---

## Dynamic Capabilities

**Core idea:** Build competence (routines) for changing routines -- meta-routines that enable adaptation rather than just execution.

Strategy in this frame is "effective management of key firm attributes that cannot be altered quickly, costlessly, or reversibly: capabilities, reputations, scale, key personnel. Strategizing is the opposite of drifting, firefighting, and myopic approaches." (Source: Sidney G. Winter, BYU-Utah Winter Strategy Conference, 2007)

**Why this matters:**
- Once routines are developed, they become semi-automatic, largely tacit, and change only slowly.
- Firms must make investment trade-offs in deciding what to "practice." Core competence, best practices, continuous improvement, quality management all reflect the routines view.
- Critical implication: firms cannot practice everything. Being good at some things means necessarily not being good at others. The choice of what to practice IS the strategic choice.
- A firm with dynamic capabilities can sense when its current routines are becoming a trap, and it has practiced the process of reconfiguring itself.

**Static capabilities vs. dynamic capabilities:**
- Static = routines for executing the current strategy well
- Dynamic = routines for changing the strategy itself -- sensing shifts, seizing opportunities, reconfiguring assets

## Radical Strategic Change (Long Jumps)

**When incremental change fails:** On a rugged landscape (high K), hill-climbing reliably traps firms on local peaks. Every nearby move looks worse. The only way to reach a higher peak is to cross a valley of lower performance.

**What long jumps look like:**
- Discontinuous, radical change in multiple attributes simultaneously
- Unlike local search, a long jump does not just tweak -- it remakes an entirely new configuration
- Examples: complete business model pivots, wholesale digital transformation, market/segment redefinition

**Risk-reward trade-off:**
- Risk: landing in a valley (the new configuration performs worse than the old one)
- Reward: reaching a significantly higher peak
- The firm must absorb a temporary performance decline during the crossing
- Long jumps are costly and uncertain, but the cost of staying trapped compounds over time

---

## When to Use Which

**Incremental change (hill-climbing)** is appropriate when:
- The landscape is smooth (low K) -- nearby moves reliably improve performance
- The firm is not yet on a local peak -- there is still room to climb
- The landscape is stable -- the fitness function is not shifting underneath the firm

**Radical change (long jump)** is appropriate when:
- The landscape is rugged (high K) -- local peaks are plentiful and traps are everywhere
- The firm is stuck on a local peak -- every small change looks worse
- The landscape is shifting -- the current peak is sinking (technology disruption, regulatory change)
- The gap between the local peak and the global peak is large enough to justify the valley crossing

**The exploration-exploitation balance:**
- Smooth landscape (low K) -> exploit (hill-climb, refine what works)
- Rugged landscape (high K) -> explore more (long jumps needed because traps are plentiful)
- The speed of landscape shift matters: fast-moving environments demand more exploration

## Internal Questions Claude Should Ask

- Does this firm have dynamic capabilities, or only static ones? Can it change its own routines, or only execute them?
- What routines would the firm need to build? How long would practice-based learning take?
- Is incremental change sufficient here, or is radical reconfiguration needed?
- What is the cost of staying trapped vs. the risk of a long jump?
- If a long jump is needed, can the firm absorb the valley crossing (financial reserves, organizational resilience, leadership commitment)?
- What has the firm chosen to practice -- and what has it therefore chosen NOT to be good at?
- Is the landscape shifting fast enough that staying put is riskier than jumping?
