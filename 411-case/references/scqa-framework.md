# SCQA Framework (Internal Reference)

> **Preamble:** SCQA is a **thinking tool**, not a presentation format. It is the first thing applied when reading a case — before generating solutions, before assumption mapping, before any analysis. The SCQA framing established in Phase 1 carries forward through the entire case and becomes the deck introduction in Phase 6.

---

## What SCQA Is

Every business case can be reduced to four elements:

| Element | Definition | What It Does |
|---------|-----------|-------------|
| **Situation** | A statement about the subject the audience agrees with | Grounds the reader in shared context |
| **Complication** | Something that twists the Situation — what changed, went wrong, or could go wrong | Creates tension and urgency |
| **Question** | The implied question raised by the Complication | Focuses the entire case |
| **Answer** | The recommendation that resolves the Question | The destination of all analysis |

**Core insight:** The mind automatically imposes order. Without a predefined framing, different listeners interpret the same facts differently. SCQA forces a single, shared interpretation before any analysis begins.

---

## How SCQA Maps to the Case Process

### Phase 1: Establish S, C, Q

The four diagnostic questions map directly to SCQA:

| Diagnostic Question | SCQA Element |
|-------------------|-------------|
| What is the history of the industry? | **Situation** — establishes the competitive context |
| What is the history of the target company? | **Situation** — establishes the firm's position and trajectory |
| What is the current situation? | **Situation** — grounds in the present moment |
| What is the core problem the firm is facing? | **Complication** — what changed, broke, or threatens the firm |

After identifying S and C, the **Question** should be made explicit: "What should the firm do about this?" or more specifically, "How should [firm] respond to [complication]?"

**Phase 1 exit gate:** S, C, and Q are confirmed with the user before proceeding.

### Phase 2: Find the A

Solution development IS finding the Answer. The selected solution completes the SCQA:
- "Our Answer to the Question is: [firm] should [solution] because [reasons]."

### Phase 6: Apply to Deck

The SCQA introduction is already established. In deck construction, format it as the opening slide sequence. No new thinking required — just formatting what was decided in Phases 1-2.

## Complication Types

Not every complication is the same. Recognizing the type helps frame the Question precisely:

| Situation Type | Complication | Implied Question |
|---|---|---|
| Recognized stable situation | Something went wrong | What do we do? |
| Recognized stable situation | Something could go wrong | How can we prevent it? |
| Recognized stable situation | Something changed | What should we do? |
| Recognized stable situation | Something could change | How should we react? |
| Here's what you might expect | Here's someone with a different view | Who is right? |
| In this situation we have alternatives | (implied) | Which one should we take? |

## Good vs. Bad SCQA

**Bad example:**
- Subject: Structuring Presentations
- S: Effective presentation skills lend credibility and clarity to a message
- C: These skills are not effectively developed during business school
- Q: How can these skills be effectively developed in business school?
- A: Professors should teach presentation skills as part of their course curricula
- **Problem:** The answer doesn't connect with the subject; the S and C don't flow logically

**Good example:**
- Subject: Structuring Presentations
- S: Effective presentations lend credibility and clarity to a message
- C: Many presentations, however, fall short by ignoring basic principles of structure
- Q: What are the principles of effective structure?
- A: Effective presentations are structured around the logic of a pyramid
- **Why it works:** The answer connects with the subject; S and C flow logically and lead directly to the implied question

### Business Case Example (Dell)
- Subject: Dell's competitive response
- S: Dell has had extraordinary results during the last few years.
- C: However, competitors have moved in to copy its business model and sales are eroding.
- Q: How should the company respond?
- A: Dell should cut costs in [x area] to drive price leadership in the online channel and add additional value-added services in [x, y, z] which competitors can't match.
- **Why it works:** Business-relevant S and C, clear causal chain from Complication to Question to Answer.

## Key Principles

- Introductions are meant to **remind** rather than to **inform**
- They should always contain the three elements of a story (S, C, Q)
- The S must leave the audience expectant for further information
- The C must twist the S in a way that creates a clear, implied Q
- The A must directly answer Q — not a tangentially related point
