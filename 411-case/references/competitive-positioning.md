## 1. Blue Ocean Strategy Canvas + ERRC Grid

**When to offer:** Solution Development phase, when considering radical alternatives or new market space.

### Red Ocean vs. Blue Ocean

| Dimension | Red Ocean (Structuralist) | Blue Ocean (Reconstructionist) |
|-----------|--------------------------|-------------------------------|
| Market boundaries | Fixed, defined by industry | Can be reshaped and reconstructed |
| Competitive logic | Fight for share within existing demand | Create new demand, make competition irrelevant |
| Strategic focus | Beat the competition | Break the value-cost trade-off |
| Value-cost relationship | Trade-off (choose differentiation OR low cost) | Pursue differentiation AND low cost simultaneously |
| View of structure | Industry structure is given; firms position within it | Firms can reshape industry structure through their actions |

The strategic shift is from a structuralist view (take the market as given, position within it) to a reconstructionist view (market boundaries are fluid, reshape them through value innovation).

### Strategy Canvas

A diagnostic and action framework for building a compelling Blue Ocean strategy.

**Methodology (4 steps):**
1. Identify all buyer-relevant features and competing factors in the industry
2. Array these factors on the horizontal axis
3. Plot competitor performance levels on each factor (low to high on vertical axis)
4. Sort the factors to highlight where competitors cluster and where strengths/weaknesses diverge

**What it reveals:**
- Where firms over-invest relative to buyer value
- Where untapped value exists (white space)
- Competitive clustering — which firms look alike
- The industry's implicit strategic logic

### ERRC Grid (Eliminate / Reduce / Raise / Create)

The four-action framework for reconstructing buyer value elements:

- **Eliminate** — factors the industry competes on but that no longer create buyer value. Strips out unnecessary cost and complexity.
- **Reduce** — factors well below industry standard where over-delivery exists. Challenges conventional wisdom about what must be offered.
- **Raise** — existing factors raised well above the industry standard. Uncovers and eliminates compromises buyers are forced to make.
- **Create** — entirely new elements the industry has never offered. Discovers new sources of buyer value and creates new demand.

### Value Innovation Mechanism

The cornerstone of Blue Ocean strategy: align utility, price, and cost to create a leap in value for both buyers and the company.

**Sustainability mechanism:** Value innovation → attracts volume → generates scale economies → raises imitation barriers. The alignment of utility, price, and cost creates a reinforcing cycle that makes imitation progressively harder.

Breaking the value-cost trade-off: instead of choosing between differentiation (high value, high cost) and cost leadership (low value, low cost), value innovation pursues both simultaneously by eliminating and reducing factors that don't create buyer value while raising and creating factors that do.

### Worked Examples

**Cirque du Soleil:**
- **Eliminated:** animal acts, star performers, three-ring format, aisle concessions
- **Reduced:** fun and humor (shifted tone), thrill and danger (reduced physical risk acts)
- **Raised/Created:** intellectual sophistication, thematic storylines, original musical scores, artistic richness, refined viewing environment
- **Result:** Attracted adults and corporate clients — an entirely new buyer group — at several times the traditional circus ticket price. Reconstructed the boundary between circus and theater.

**Ford Model T:**
- **Eliminated:** customization, multiple colors ("any color so long as it's black"), handmade luxury, model variety
- **Reduced:** styling, optional features, dealer negotiation
- **Raised/Created:** reliability, durability, ease of use, ease of maintenance, mass production via assembly line, standardized parts
- **Result:** "A car for the great multitude" at a fraction of the industry price. Reconstructed the boundary between luxury carriages and practical transportation.

**Southwest Airlines:**
- **Eliminated:** meals, lounges, hub-and-spoke routing, seating choice, interline baggage transfers, first/business class
- **Reduced:** ticket price (dramatically)
- **Raised/Created:** frequency of departures, speed (point-to-point), friendly service, gate turnaround time
- **Result:** Competed not just with other airlines but with car travel. Created demand among people who previously drove rather than flew. Value curve diverges sharply from traditional carriers on the strategy canvas.

## 2. Positional Differentiation

**When to offer:** When the firm needs to define or refine its competitive position.

### Five Dimensions of Differentiation

1. **Price** — where the firm sits on the price spectrum
2. **Cost** — structural cost position relative to rivals
3. **Service** — quality, responsiveness, and scope of service delivery
4. **Technology** — technical capability, innovation, platform sophistication
5. **Other Features** — brand, design, convenience, distribution, customization

### How Differentiation Works

- Differentiation provides an exclusive set of buyers who uniquely prefer the firm's offering over all alternatives
- The firm occupies a specific position in attribute space — a particular configuration of the five dimensions
- Advantage comes from choosing a configuration that (a) enough buyers value and (b) rivals cannot easily replicate

### Risk of Erosion

Positional differentiation erodes as:
- Buyer preferences change (demand shifts to different configurations)
- Rivals imitate (closing the gap on differentiating attributes)
- Innovation emerges (new attributes redefine what matters)

Adapting position as the landscape moves = incremental improvement (hill-climbing). Only works if demand for the alternative configuration actually exists.

### Connection to Mobility Barriers

When positional differentiation becomes deeply entrenched (high K — many interdependent choices reinforcing the position), it creates **mobility barriers** that define strategic groups. Entrenched differentiation (high K) creates intra-industry mobility barriers — like entry barriers, but between strategic groups rather than between industries. This is why strategic groups persist: the cost of reconfiguring from one position to another is prohibitively high when many interdependent choices must change simultaneously.

### Hill-Climbing Analogy

Positional adjustment is hill-climbing on the fitness landscape: small moves along attribute dimensions, accepting only changes that improve competitive fitness. Effective in smooth terrain (low K), but potentially trapping in rugged terrain (high K) where the firm converges on a local peak.
